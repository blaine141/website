Robotics Scouting

February 2017 - March 2017

       Last year, Liz Halter from Olentangy High school with the help of her dad created an interesting scouting system. We never really did any scouting before created her system that year. She made a program hosted from a computer that other devices would connect to through a wired network. It would then make decisions about matches from that data collected. We won an award that year for our scouting system. I didn't help her make or operate any of it so that is about as much as I know. 

        This year, 2017, I wanted to follow in her footsteps, so early in the year I started developing software. I was in AP Statistics so I decided to make it all fancy. I added probability of win calculations and all other sorts of features. 

        About two weeks later I heard other people saying that they wanted to work on scouting. I joined in on the conversation and we started from scratch. The original team was me, Ryan Schwartz, Will Main, and the adult mentor Patrick Worth. We had a series of meetings where we discussed objectives and end goals. It was a pretty good experience to be working on a team like that brainstorming. Mr. Worth had worked with this web service called Drupal. It allows data structures and... I don't quite know the name. Anyway Mr. Worth had experience and he shared it with us. 

        Once we had all the ideas on the board, we started eliminating and narrowing down onto what we wanted. We finally came to the solution. We would have a web app, a website that you can cache and run as an app. We would then share this app with other teams and have them collect data, then share some of our resources with them. Gracious professionalism, guys. Ryan Schwartz was in charge of the app. How it would work is you record auto data, amount of fuel scored, starting position, whether or not a gear was scored, whether there is a gear in the not, whether the line was crossed, etc. Then, during teleop, you would record when events happened in the match. When the bot picked up a gear, you would click the gear start button. When it was delivered, you would select stop and select whether or not the gear was delivered successfully, effectively creating a timeline of the match. Then at the end of the match you can record notes and show where the bot usually traveled. The team scout recorded all that information. Now in the brainstorming process we concluded that It would be near impossible to watch the whole match and also keep track of fuel score. So we had another type of scout, the fuel scout. They recorded how the fuel score changed for each alliance during the match. The app also had features for assigning certain people to specific matches. Ryan really put focus on making the app look nice with Google's material design. I have to admit, it looks nice, and he even showed me how to incorporate the design in my slideshow. This page has some info on the app.

        I worked on the data storage website and the analytics. As I said earlier, the website was made with Drupal. I made data structures and Mr. Worth taught me how to make modules using PHP. I made modules to take JSON formatted input and create the data structures to represent that information. From the website you could view each match and info about them. From that point the data was exported through JSON and downloaded to be analyzed.

        My analysis program was created in Visual Basic. It looked at the events recorded and then converted that to useful data as simple as average gears per match to the usual amount of time taken to score one gear to the accuracy of the shooter. To know how well the shooters scored, it matched up each change in the fuel score to a team by looking at when each team was shooting. All of this data was then compiled down into team and match info. For each match it looked at how much time it took for them to score fuel and gears and then calculated how many gears and fuel each team should score for a highest alliance score. Then, for each team, data was exported into CSV files. One file contained general summary information for each team, one contained each event from each match for each team, and finally, one contained info on where the team usually drives their bot. Another file contained match optimization info.

        All of this data meant nothing if you didn't have a way to look at it. Liz used Tableau, a data visualization software last year, so we decided to go down the same route. We made a sheet for team info and a sheet for match info. You just selected which team or match you want and it will show you all the data for that team or match.         Before each match we would just print off the match dashboard for each alliance and brief our alliance on what we were going to see in the next match. 

        One other thing I wanted to make was a slideshow in the pit area that will show summaries of scouting data. Some of the slides that were included were the top three climbers, shooters, and gear teams. It also included a slide for match predictions for upcoming matches and the "Dream Alliance". All of this could be easily updated by plugging a flash drive in the laptop which triggers it to copy all of the scouting info off and update the slideshow. Pretty cool, huh?

        I have to say, it was quite an experience even though I spent my whole spring break coding.
